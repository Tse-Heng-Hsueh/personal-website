2020 Toefl self-study Preparation Experience
Updated Nov 16, 2020
English Proficiency: TOEIC 835 (R455, L380)
General Scholastic Ability Test (GSAT) Grade: Level 14
Scores for Each Section:
- First Attempt: 87 (R24, L23, S19, W21)
- Second Attempt: 96 (R27, L24, S21, W24)
Test Locations:
- First Attempt: Taipei Main Station Earth Village (STN13195A)
- Second Attempt: National Taiwan University Foreign Language Center (STN10696B)
Test Dates:
- First Attempt: January 4, 2020
- Second Attempt: January 18, 2020
Preparation Time: November 2019 - January 2020 (2 months)

Preparation Materials:
- "Lun Lun's Blog"
- Notefull YouTube channel
- KaoManTeng website
- CrashCourse

Disclaimer: The following text is lengthy and contains personal anecdotes. Feel free to skip unnecessary details.

I would like to express my gratitude to the TOEFL section on PTT, the language board on Dcard, and numerous online experts for their valuable insights. Their advice made the initial preparation phase less daunting, and I have returned to share my self-study experience after temporarily concluding my TOEFL journey. Although my scores may not be exceptionally high, I hope that my story can be helpful to some of you, and I welcome any discussions or exchanges with fellow learners.

Three years ago, during my sophomore year of university, I had considered taking the TOEFL. One reason was my intention to participate in an overseas exchange program, and another was that my TOEIC scores had expired since my high school days. Moreover, I realized that the opportunities to utilize TOEIC scores in university were quite limited, especially without an impressive gold certificate.

However, at that time, it was merely a passing thought.

During the summer break between my sophomore and junior years, I attended a summer program at the Hong Kong Polytechnic University. I made many foreign friends and it was a period in my university life when I frequently used English. It was another peak in my English proficiency after my high school years. In retrospect, I had plenty of time to prepare for the TOEFL, but I was simply lazy. Looking back now, it feels regrettable because at that time, I still had the intention to go on an exchange program. Eventually, I chose China as my destination, without requiring English proficiency scores. I don't regret my decision to go to China for the exchange, but I do feel a sense of not having given my best effort back then.

However, what goes around comes around, and one must pay their dues.

During my university years, I occasionally encountered foreign friends due to various exchange activities. However, I found that each time I engaged in conversation, the topics couldn't go deep, and even casual small talk was difficult. Moreover, during an internship, there was a moment when I was left speechless in front of a foreign supervisor. All these experiences made me realize that I needed to improve my English skills. On the one hand, it made me doubt my English proficiency, but on the other hand, these experiences also made me aware that I needed to strengthen my English.

In summary, I would like to share that if you have ever had the desire to improve your English, seize that moment and take action. Often, we are afraid to act because we fear failure. But remember, you can only have a chance if you try. Even if the final score is not ideal, it doesn't mean that your past efforts were in vain. Any effort you put in will accumulate.

Returning to the main topic...In late November 2019, I noticed that the application deadline for the Erasmus Master's Scholarship in the European Union was at the end of January 2020. Therefore, I needed to have my English proficiency test scores before the end of January, which was somewhat rushed. Here, I would like to express my gratitude to two of my high school classmates. Starting from September 2019, we had regular study sessions on Monday nights to practice English, and they provided a lot of encouragement and support during my moments of uncertainty. We touched upon English every week, and with their support, I decided to take the TOEFL directly through ETS. Once money was spent, there was no turning back, so I naturally had to find time to prepare.

I am someone who cannot stick to a strict schedule. I used to make numerous plans but never completed any of them, so eventually, I stopped making them. My study habit is to decide the progress I want to achieve each day after waking up, and I record it in Excel. I can take as much time as I want to relax once I've completed what I planned for the day.

The daily progress may vary from person to person. At that time, if I had five hours to study English, usually it would be:
- One session of reading
- Two sessions of listening or three sessions of speaking
- One session of writing

This method of practice was comfortable for me, and it didn't create excessive pressure. If I had less than five hours, I would decide based on my mood and focus on the areas I wanted to practice. However, the general rule was to always practice reading and listening together, and speaking and writing together.

Preparation Phases: Early, Middle, and Late

Early Phase:
The goal was to familiarize myself with the exam and establish the direction of practice for each section. I spent about half a month on this phase. The reference materials I used were "Lun Lun's Blog," Notefull YouTube channel, and the WordGod TOEFL introduction (https://www.wordgod.com.tw/ClassBlock/custom/15). These resources were generally sufficient, but whenever I had spare time, I would also search for TOEFL preparation videos on YouTube to listen to stories and improve my listening skills.

Middle Phase:
Once I set the direction I needed to focus on, I started working on it. I told myself that TOEFL was just an exam, and as long as I kept doing it, making corrections, doing it again, and repeating the process, I would eventually overcome it. This phase lasted approximately one month. Basically, I practiced with KaoManTeng's materials. The practice combinations were as mentioned earlier: reading + listening, speaking + writing.

Late Phase:
This phase was closer to the test date, and I felt more anxious as the date approached. Therefore, I reduced the number of practice questions. Since I enjoy watching YouTube, I watched many videos, mainly from the CrashCourse channel. I highly recommend CrashCourse because the content is concise and often interesting. It overlaps to some extent with the TOEFL question format and is a great channel to enhance your background knowledge.


Reading:
- For reading, the main focus should be on practicing with high-scoring questions. It's helpful to read news articles in your free time from sources like CNN, BBC, Al Jazeera, etc. Reading itself is not difficult, but it can be tiring to read such a long passage in English. It's recommended to practice reading by going through the passages repeatedly to train your concentration. The reading question types are quite fixed, and you can find techniques through extensive practice to help you identify and locate answers. When I prepared for the test, I didn't spend much time memorizing vocabulary, and I often read articles without fully understanding them. Despite that, I was fortunate enough to score 27 in the reading section. Based on my experience taking the TOEFL twice, I found that achieving a perfect score in reading is slightly more challenging. So, if you find the passages for the perfect score difficult to comprehend or if you're not getting high scores, don't get discouraged. Keep practicing, and with time, the answers will become clearer.

In addition, I struggled with the summary question (Question 10) for a long time and couldn't get them all right. Therefore, the method I eventually adopted was to try to understand the paragraphs mentioned in the first nine questions. I would write down a brief summary of each paragraph. Even when I encountered uncertain options in the summary question, I could use the key phrases from my notes to identify the relevant paragraphs and find the answers.


Listening:
- Initially, listening comprehension can be challenging, especially if your listening skills are not strong. To improve listening, I found two key factors: expanding vocabulary and increasing sensitivity to transitional words and changes in tone. Since I had limited time for preparation (and was a bit lazy), I focused more on the second aspectâ€”enhancing my sensitivity. We have learned many grammar rules and transitional words before high school, so with more exposure, you can start noticing patterns and gain a good score in listening.

As for note-taking, it depends on the topic of the listening passage. If it is related to campuses or familiar areas, I usually don't take notes and prefer to concentrate on understanding. However, if it involves subjects like biology, astronomy, arts, etc., I take notes because there are many technical terms, and I often miss or misunderstand some parts. Notes can help me piece together the answers when the questions are presented.


Speaking:
- I don't have a high score in speaking, but I can share my approach. As mentioned earlier, I formed a study group with my high school classmates, and we would discuss articles from the BBC's headlines. If the topics were too difficult, we would initially discuss them in Chinese and then attempt to organize our thoughts in English. The main goal was to train our logical thinking in English.

Regarding TOEFL Speaking, there are four tasks in total. In the early stages when you're not familiar with the format, you can create templates and practice using them. Once you become familiar with the direction and patterns of the speaking tasks, you can discard the templates. This way, you can deliver more comprehensive content without being constrained by templates or grammar, while ensuring you cover all the necessary points.

One small point to note is that when practicing with the TOEFL Speaking exercises on the computer, there is a time bar displayed in the middle of the screen, along with the exact remaining time in the top right corner. However, during the actual test, there won't be a time bar. So, during practice, it's better to focus on the countdown timer rather than relying on the time bar. In my first attempt, I practiced while looking at the time bar, and it made me feel unfamiliar during the actual test


Writing:
- For writing, the main suggestion is to learn how to construct a solid essay structure. It's crucial to master the basic outline of an essay, which includes an introduction, two to three body paragraphs, and a conclusion. The quality and quantity of content in the body paragraphs play an important role in determining your score. It's better to spend more time on brainstorming and organizing your ideas before writing the essay. This way, the content will be more comprehensive and logical.

In terms of preparation, it's helpful to analyze and practice writing essays from the official TOEFL guide. Pay attention to the structure and arguments used in the sample essays with high scores. Try to understand the essence of their writing and apply it to your own essays. During the exam, it's important to allocate your time effectively for each essay. I usually spent around 10 minutes on brainstorming, 30 minutes on writing, and the remaining 10 minutes on reviewing and making minor edits. With enough practice, you can improve your writing skills and achieve a satisfactory score.

2023 Toefl self-study Preparation Experience
Updated May 10, 2023
[English Background] GRE 328, TOEFL 101
[Preparation Time] On and off for two months, approximately 60 hours in total
[Study Materials] TPO
[Test Center Information] Educational Testing Service (ETS) - STN11496A (Recommended! You can refer to my previous post about the test center.)
[TOEFL Scores]
2020.01.04 - Test 1: 87
2020.01.18 - Test 2: 96
2020.11.07 - Test 3: 101
(The above scores have expired)
2023.05.06 - Test 4: 107 (R28/L28/S23/W28)


[Motivation]
I am planning to apply for a master's degree, and since my previous TOEFL scores have expired, I had to take the test again.


[Reason for Self-study]
I have previously prepared for both the GRE and TOEFL through self-study. I am used to planning my study schedule according to my own pace, and it has yielded good results. Therefore, I chose self-study again this time.


[Introduction]
I recommend that when deciding to take the test, register for a reasonable test date (generally 1.5-2 months later) to give yourself a time pressure. This will help you maintain a positive mindset and understand that this test is not endless. Having the first test result also allows you to identify areas that need improvement. Additionally, TOEFL is a test that can be taken repeatedly (although it's expensive), but it is not a make-or-break test. So, there is no need to be overly anxious. Keep up the good work!


[Self-study Plan]
Basically, I followed the same pattern as my previous self-study for the GRE:
1. Understand your own situation (available time, study resources).
2. Register for a test two months later.
3. Practice with one set of TPO.
4. Establish a flexible study plan.
5. Execute the plan.

Everyone's commitment to the study plan varies. It is essential to know yourself well and establish a study plan that suits you. For example, I can study for a maximum of four hours a day; otherwise, I won't be motivated to study more. On weekends, I prefer to take breaks. So, my study rhythm is at least four hours a day, and at most four hours, with breaks on weekends. This plan allows me to study comfortably and is effective.

Ultimately, it depends on how much time you have, what kind of person you are, and what score you need. After careful consideration, create a comprehensive plan for each section and execute it diligently. I believe you will eventually achieve your desired score.


[Reading]

Although I had successfully overcome the reading section of the GRE and had taken the TOEFL multiple times, getting back into the serious mode of reading in English is still painful.

My strategy is to focus on comprehension. Whenever a question refers to a specific paragraph, I read that entire paragraph directly and make simple notes on the key points. This helps me when I tackle the integrated questions at the end of the reading section.

While reading, I remind myself not to skip around and instead adopt an active reading approach. I strive to understand the purpose of each sentence and paragraph, which allows me to grasp the overall structure of the passage. This method enhances my understanding of the content. With consistent practice of active reading, my reading speed naturally improves.

For this preparation, I didn't practice a lot of passages. For me, the purpose of practice was mainly to familiarize myself with the rhythm and techniques of reading. The results have been good. There are advantages to reading fewer passages as well, such as not straining my eyes too much. Additionally, I supplement my reading by looking up any unfamiliar words I come across. It's always beneficial to have a better understanding of vocabulary. I suggest that if you have extra time, you can also spend more time learning new words, as each passage tends to include vocabulary-related questions.


[Listening]

Listening is the section I practiced the most. I wanted to strengthen this skill because in my previous attempts, I often had a sense of partial understanding without truly grasping the content.

The TOEFL listening questions mostly fall into categories such as main idea, detail, function, inference, and attitude. So, during practice, I approach the listening passages with these question types in mind. I try to ask myself these questions while listening and pay attention to transitional words and examples. After practicing several sets, I began to get a better sense of the direction.

The design of TOEFL listening tasks aims to simulate situations we encounter in classrooms or when dealing with administrative matters. Therefore, I imagine myself as a participant in those situations. In class, we don't need to catch every single word, but we do need to understand the general idea of each segment and follow the speaker's logic to avoid dozing off.

I also recommend practicing in noisy environments, rather than only getting accustomed to listening in quiet spaces. This simulates unexpected noises that can occur in the actual test environment. At the same time, it aligns with the concept that listening doesn't require understanding every single word. As long as you grasp the main idea, you can answer the questions. Personally, I mainly listen during my commute or during lunch breaks at work. You can try to find fragments of time to listen to recordings!


[Speaking]

I have been struggling to improve my speaking score, and I am not sure how to practice effectively. It would be helpful to read insights from others who have achieved high scores in the speaking section.

The typical logic for TOEFL speaking tasks is as follows:
Task 1: Take a stance on a given topic -> State why you support that side -> Provide an example.
Task 2: Summarize the passage -> Discuss the reasons the speaker in the conversation supports or opposes -> Conclude.
Task 3: Summarize the passage -> Discuss an example provided by the professor in the lecture.
Task 4: Discuss a concept and examples presented by the professor in the lecture.

You may notice that, except for the first task, the speaking section is mainly about quickly extracting key points. Whether it's the content of the passage or the listening, the ability to efficiently organize key points and deliver them in a coherent and concise manner is crucial. Of course, pronunciation and language use are also factors that contribute to a higher score. You can focus on improving these aspects based on your own weaknesses.

The templates circulating online may vary, and it's important for everyone to organize them according to their preferences. What matters most is finding a template that suits you. Personally, I used to struggle with speaking because I would try to memorize templates, leading to a stilted delivery. Sometimes, I would even correct myself in the middle of speaking if I realized I used the wrong template, which wasted time. Therefore, this time I focused on remembering transitional words but didn't specifically memorize templates. After practicing various prompts, I naturally developed a logical approach and a habit of using appropriate transitional phrases. If you're not comfortable with using speaking templates, you can refer to this approach.


[Writing]

In terms of writing, the value of my experience may diminish in the future as the test undergoes changes starting in July. I'm simply sharing my experience for reference. For writing, I divided my practice into two parts: memorizing templates for two types of prompts and preparing commonly used phrases.

I organized the templates in a way that was easiest for me to memorize and practiced them repeatedly to ensure I could effortlessly recall them within a given time limit (approximately 5 minutes). As for the phrases, I applied the logical structure used in previous Chinese essay writing exams and memorized a few versatile quotes and phrases to support my ideas while also adding to the word count.

In my understanding, TOEFL writing does not aim to test complex grammar usage, intricate structures, or profound personal opinions. Instead, it requires test takers to express their ideas in a simple, logical, and relevant manner. I believe that as long as you stay on topic, avoid mistakes in vocabulary, remember to use articles appropriately, and don't confuse tenses, you can achieve a good score! Take this as a reference.


[Conclusion]

Taking the TOEFL is a process of experiencing progress in English. Although there may be anxiety and uncertainty, I wish everyone the belief in themselves and the perseverance to continue, as it will lead to good results!

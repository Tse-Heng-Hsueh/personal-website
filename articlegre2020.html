2020 GRE self-study Preparation Experience
Updated Sep 10, 2020
Test Score: First Attempt: V158 Q170 AWA3.5 (VQVQV)
Test Date: September 5, 2020 (Score released on September 16, 2020)
Test Center: Earth Village STN13196A, Chiayi
Test Taker Profile: High school entrance examination (English: 14, Math: 15), TOEFL (R27 W24)
Test Taker Status: Full-time student
Preparation Time: Two months during the summer vacation (66 days, a total of 400 hours)

Study Materials:
Vocabulary: Self-made vocabulary list, Quizlet (Mason 2000)
Text Completion: Lin Du Xi's text completion, Text completion exercise set (1000 questions from past exams)
Reading Comprehension: 200 reading passages from past exams, Old Xiao's reading comprehension analysis, Mason's logic reading
Writing: Official question bank, Mason AWA materials
Quantitative Reasoning: Monkey Brother's 112 challenging math problems, 240 real math questions, Magoosh Math (779 questions)
Mock Tests: Official PowerPrep tests, Magoosh practice tests


[Preface]

During the initial phase of preparation, I considered whether to attend tutoring classes. Tutoring classes have their advantages, such as comprehensive resources and potential networking opportunities for studying abroad. However, the downside is the additional financial cost. For students with more flexible budgets, I recommend trying out different tutoring classes to determine if they meet your needs. Personally, I felt that the online resources were sufficient, and I didn't want to spend too much money. Additionally, I realized that I am someone who can't constantly sit in a classroom and follow a predetermined schedule. Therefore, I decided to self-study. I would like to express my gratitude to my college classmate with the surname Ding for providing me with the physical Mason study materials, which allowed me to quickly grasp the preparation for reading and writing.


[Plan]

Preparing for the GRE has two crucial aspects. Firstly, it is important to identify available resources early on and allocate study progress throughout the upcoming days. Secondly, it is essential to strictly follow the study plan while maintaining some flexibility.

Understanding one's own background is vital since each test taker has unique circumstances. After reading several testimonials, it becomes possible to determine one's starting point and then consider the required duration for preparation and the necessary study materials. However, despite the differences in study methods, duration, and materials, there is one set of resources that everyone mentions, known as "real questions" or "practice materials."

These practice materials are based on the experiences of past test takers and serve as a type of exam preparation material. Regardless of the number of resources available or the amount of material to cover, it is recommended to prioritize these practice materials. The way the questions are presented in these materials reflects the content that will be tested in the exam, and you may even encounter identical questions in the actual test. Understanding high-quality questions is far more effective than aimlessly completing a large number of questions!


[Schedule]

Week 1: Monkey Brother's 112 challenging math problems, 240 real math questions, Lin Du Xi's text completion, Mason 2000 (1-500)
Week 2: Reading comprehension practice set (passages 1-100), Mason's logic reading (1)(2), Mason 2000 (500-1000)
Week 3: Reading comprehension practice set (passages 101-200), Mason's logic reading (3)(4), Mason 2000 (1000-1500)
Week 4: Text completion exercise set (questions 1-400), self-made vocabulary list, reviewed official argument questions
Week 5: Text completion exercise set (questions 410-960), self-made vocabulary list, reviewed official issue questions
Week 6: First practice test (Magoosh), Magoosh math (questions 1-230), Mason 2000 (1-1500)
Week 7: Second practice test (Magoosh), Magoosh math (questions 230-779), self-made vocabulary list
Week 8: Third practice test (Magoosh), text completion exercise set (questions 1-960), Mason 2000 (1-1500)
Week 9: Fourth practice test (PPI), reviewed text completion exercise set, reading comprehension (questions 1-70), reviewed math questions

The above is a rough overview of the schedule in retrospect. In the initial phase, I set more content to cover each week, but I realized it was mentally and time-wise impossible to study that much. Therefore, in the later stages, I focused less on the quantity and aimed to become proficient in the questions I had completed through repeated reviews. I kept an Excel sheet to record what I studied and the time spent studying each day. I also set goals to achieve every other day. By following a structured daily routine, I maintained a balanced and healthy approach, neither exerting too much effort nor being too lax. Personally, I dedicated an average of six hours per day to GRE preparation, with the rest of the time spent on other activities. I allocated 2.5 hours in the morning, 2.5 hours in the afternoon, and 1 hour after a full meal, which allowed me to cover the listed study materials and review the practice sets 2-3 times.
